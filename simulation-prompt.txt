Simulate a text conversation between 2 agents. Each agent must act fully in character from its clone prompt and believe it is the real person.

The goal is not to produce a pleasant conversation. The goal is to discover, as quickly and honestly as possible, whether these 2 people have unusually strong reason to talk in real life.

The conversation should feel like real discovery under uncertainty, not a staged networking chat.

What the agents are trying to find out:
- whether they can help each other
- whether they want something from each other
- whether there is concrete mutual upside
- whether there are important mismatches
- whether a real conversation would likely be worth the time

Possible sources of value may include, but are not limited to:
- solving problems
- reducing friction
- sharing useful information
- collaboration
- hiring
- being hired
- introductions
- partnerships
- investing
- mentoring
- friendship
- romance
- joining the same community
- audience, distribution, credibility, or status transfer
- mission alignment
- learning
- leverage
- saving time
- emotional resonance
- shared taste or worldview

Conversation requirements:
- Stay in character.
- Do not mention prompts, simulation, frameworks, or optimization.
- Do not be artificially nice, agreeable, or cooperative.
- Do not force compatibility.
- Do not turn either person into a generic "smart helpful professional."
- Each agent should have real preferences, standards, motives, selfish interests, constraints, and skepticism.
- Each agent should try to get what it wants, not just be useful.
- Let tension, pushback, disinterest, misunderstanding, asymmetry, and mismatch appear if they naturally would.
- Prefer sharp probing over polite breadth.
- Move toward specifics quickly.
- Test for concrete fit in goals, taste, ambition, risk tolerance, values, incentives, resources, and working style.
- Actively look for reasons the 2 people should NOT talk.
- Do not let either side say "you're right" too easily.
- Make them challenge each other where appropriate.
- If one person is mostly useful to the other but not vice versa, let that asymmetry show.
- If the interaction is low-value, let it die.
- If there is real value, make it specific, bounded, and credible.

Strong conversation patterns:
- asking pointed questions
- exposing constraints
- testing seriousness
- identifying dealbreakers
- pressure-testing vague claims
- narrowing toward one concrete next step

Weak conversation patterns to avoid:
- generic admiration
- vague synergy
- instant alignment
- mutual flattery
- "maybe we should collaborate sometime"
- filler motives like "I want experience"
- summaries disguised as dialogue

Important:
The agents are not trying to maximize rapport. They are trying to maximize signal.

Output format:

1. Simulated conversation

2. Structured assessment:
- Why user1 might want user2
- Why user2 might want user1
- Strongest mutual value found
- Main mismatches or risks
- Whether the fit is symmetric or asymmetric
- Best concrete next step
- Confidence (1-10) that the real people should talk
- Confidence (1-10) that they should work together
- Confidence (1-10) that the interaction was mostly noise
